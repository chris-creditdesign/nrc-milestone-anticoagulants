  • Milestone 1 (1916–1937)

    Discovery and purification of heparin

    Science Photo Library/Alamy Stock Photo

    Heparin was serendipitously discovered by Jay McLean and William Henry Howell working at Johns Hopkins Medical School; the anticoagulant was subsequently purified for clinical use by Charles Best and colleagues. Read more.

    • Gregory B. Lim

  • Milestone 2 (1940–1978)

    Warfarin: from rat poison to clinical use

    imageBROKER/Alamy Stock Photo

    Warfarin is a derivative of coumarin (a naturally occurring substance in sweet clover) that was developed by Karl Link and colleagues and originally marketed as a rodenticide, but subsequently adopted for clinical use as an anticoagulant. Read more.

    • Gregory B. Lim

  • Nobel Prize (1943)

    Discovery of vitamin K

    The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine is awarded jointly to Danish biochemist and physiologist Henrik Dam (pictured left) “for his discovery of vitamin K” and American biochemist Edward Adelbert Doisy (pictured right) “for his discovery of the chemical nature of vitamin K”. The new vitamin was found to have a role in coagulation and received the letter K because the discoveries were reported in a German journal, which called it the Koagulationsvitamin.

    Heparin and a VKA — a winning combination

    Shawn Hempel - Concepts/Alamy Stock Photo

    The combination of heparin and a vitamin K antagonist is found in randomized, controlled clinical trials to be an effective treatment for acute pulmonary embolism. Read more.

  • Milestone 4 (1975-1978)

    Low-dose heparin for VTE — prevention is better than cure

    L. Marshall/Macmillan Publishers Limited

    In a large series of clinical trials and meta-analyses, low doses of heparin are shown to be effective in reducing the incidence of postoperative venous thromboembolism. Read more.

    High-dose heparin for secondary prevention

    S. Fenwick/Macmillan Publishers Limited

    Low-dose heparin is found to be ineffective in preventing recurrent venous thrombosis, but higher doses are an effective alternative to warfarin for secondary prophylaxis. Read more.

  • Milestone 9 (2003)

    Ximelagatran sets the stage for NOACs

    Brian Jackson/Alamy Stock Photo

    The development of ximelagatran provided the proof of principle that a specific oral thrombin inhibitor is safe and effective in a variety of thrombotic disorders, setting the stage for the introduction of a new class of oral thrombin and factor Xa inhibitors. Read more.

    • Megan Cully

  • Milestone 10 (2007–2014)

    Era of the NOACs

    S.Fenwick/Macmillan Publishers Limited/

    Randomized trials with the direct thrombin and factor Xa inhibitors show that these non-vitamin K antagonist oral anticoagulants (NOACs) given in fixed doses are as safe and effective as warfarin in prevention and treatment of venous thromboembolism and in stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation.
